Let $f: A \to B$ be a function. $A$ <b>left inverse</b> of $f$ is a function $g: B \to A$ such that $g \circ f = i_A$
	Left inverses are not unique, meaning there can be more than one

Let $f: A \to B$ be a function. A <b>right inverse</b> of $f$ is a function $g: B \to A$ such that $f \circ g = i_B$

In general, a function can have a left inverse or a right inverse, or both, or neither

Let $f: A \to B$ be a function.  If $f$ has a left inverse $l: B \to A$ and a right inverse $r: B \to A$, then $l = r$

Let $f: A \to B$ be a function. The inverse(or two-sided inverse) of $f$ is a function $g: B \to A$ that is a left and right inverse of $f$
	By the above theorem, if a function $f$ has an inverse, then it is unique. We denote the inverse of $f$ by $f^{-1}$ 

If $f: A \to B$ has an inverse $f^{-1}$, then $f(x) = y \iff x = f^{-1}(y)$ 
	We can use this,$f(x) = y \iff x = f^{-1}(y)$, to solve $f(x) =y$ for $x$

Let $f: A \to B$ be a function
	$f$ is injective $\iff$ $f$ has a left inverse
	$f$ is surjective $\iff$ $f$ has a right inverse
	$f$ is bijective $\iff$ $f$ has an inverse
