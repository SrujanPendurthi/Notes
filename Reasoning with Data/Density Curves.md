Area = proportion of observations = relative frequency = probability

Density Curves as the Probability Distribution of a Continuous Random Variable is an idealized curve, understood to represent the frequency distribution
	always above the x-axis
	is scaled so total area under the curve is 1, therefore, area measures probability

Mean is denoted $\mu$ and standard deviation is denoted $\sigma$ 

Common Elementary Distributions of Continuous Random Variables:
	Exponential Distributions(curves)
	Uniform Distributions(flat lines)
	Normal Distributions(bell curves) also called Gaussian Distribution

Normal Distributions are important because they often model the sorts of variables that the results of mixing many independent factors seen in the natural world, while modeling certain features of samples that are randomly selected

Some characteristics of Normal Distribution Curves
	Infinite family of density curves whose shape is like $y = e^{-x^2}$ 
	Symmetric, unimodal
	Mouth-down
	Bell shaped
	Mean is at the center due to symmetry
	Standard deviation is the horizontal distance between the two inflection points of the curve

67% fall in the first standard deviation
95% fall within 2 standard deviations
99.7% fall within 3 standard deviations

The standardized score(or Z score) is the number of standard deviations from the mean 
	the normal density curve with mean of 0 and standard deviation of 1 has its scores already standardized
	Example: IQ, Mean is 100 and every standard deviation is 10 points

$Z = \frac {\text{observation minus mean}}{\text{standard deviation}}$

The Standard Normal distribution is a natural reference for discussing probability on all other Normal distributions, because on all Normal curves, portions corresponding to the same Z score have the same area
